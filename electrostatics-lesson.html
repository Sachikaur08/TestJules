Chapter Explanation

1. Electric Charge

1.1 Definition

Charge is that property that is associated with the matter due to which it produces and experiences electrical and magnetic effects.

1.2 Type

There exist two types of charges in nature. They are:

  • Positive charge
  • Negative charge

Charges with the same electrical sign repel each other while charges with opposite electrical signs attract each other.

1.3 Unit and Dimensional Formula

S.I. unit of charge is coulomb (C),

1mC = 10⁻³ C, 1 μC = 10⁻⁶ C, 1nC = 10⁻⁹ C

C.G.S. unit of charge is e.s.u.

1C = 3 × 10⁹ esu

Dimensional formula is given by [Q] = [AT].

1.4 Point Charge

Whose spatial size is negligible as compared to other distances.

1.5 Properties of Charge

  • (i) Charge is a Scalar Quantity: Charges can be added or subtracted algebraically.
  • (ii) Charge is transferable: When a charged body is put in contact with an uncharged body, the uncharged body becomes charged due to the transfer of electrons.
  • (iii) Charge is always associated with mass: Charge cannot exist without mass though mass can exist without charge.
  • (iv) Charge is conserved: Charge can neither be created nor destroyed.
  • (v) Invariance of charge: The numerical value of an elementary charge is independent of velocity.
  • (vi) Charge produces an electric and magnetic field: A rest charge produces only electric field; a moving charge produces both electric and magnetic fields; accelerated charge also radiates energy as EM waves.
  • (vii) Charge resides on the surface of conductor: Like charges repel, so charges move to outer surface. Thus solid and hollow conducting spheres of the same radius hold equal maximum charge.
  • (viii) Quantization of charge: Charge appears in discrete values, i.e., Q=±ne, where e=1.6×10−19 C and n=0,1,2,3…

1.6 Comparison of Charge and Mass

S. No Charge Mass
1 Can be positive, negative or zero Always positive
2 Independent of velocity Increases with velocity (relativistically)
3 Quantized Quantization not yet established
4 Conserved Not conserved (mass-energy interconversion)
5 Can be attractive or repulsive Always attractive (gravitational)

1.7 Methods of Charging

  • (i) By friction: Electrons are transferred from one body to another upon rubbing, creating opposite charges (e.g., glass + silk, ebonite + wool).
  • (ii) By induction: A charged body near a neutral one induces opposite charge on the near side and similar charge on the far side. The inducing body loses no charge.
  • (iii) By conduction: A charged conductor transfers charge to another when brought in contact. Both get similarly charged.

2. Coulomb's Law

If two stationary and point charges q₁ and q₂ are kept at a distance r, the force F between them is:

F = k × (q₁ × q₂) / r²

Where k is a constant.

In SI units:

k = 8.988 × 10⁹ N·m²/C² ≈ 9.0 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²

Direction of force is along the line joining the charges.

Force is repulsive if charges are alike, attractive if opposite.

Force is conservative and follows the inverse square law.

2.1 Variation of k

In C.G.S. units (air):

F = (Q₁ × Q₂) / r², and k = 1, unit is dyne.

In S.I. units:

k = 1 / (4 × π × ε₀) = 9 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²

Where ε₀ (epsilon naught) is the absolute permittivity of free space:

ε₀ = 8.85 × 10⁻¹² C²/N·m²

Dimensions of ε₀:

[M⁻¹ L⁻³ T⁴ A²]

Also,

c = 1 / √(μ₀ × ε₀)

where μ₀ is the magnetic permeability of free space and c is the speed of light.

2.1.2 Effect of Medium

If a dielectric is present between the charges, the force becomes:

F_medium = F_air / K

or,

F = (1 / (4 × π × ε₀ × K)) × (q₁ × q₂) / r²

Where K = dielectric constant = ε_r = relative permittivity of the medium.

Common values of K:

  • Vacuum/Air = 1
  • Water = 80
  • Mica = 6
  • Glass = 5 to 10
  • Metal = ∞

3. Electric Field

Electric field is the region around a charge where another charge experiences a force.

3.1 Electric Field Intensity (E)

Defined as the force experienced by a unit positive test charge placed at a point.

E = F / q₀

(Here q₀ → 0 to avoid disturbing the source charge)

Unit: N/C or V/m

Dimension: [M L T⁻³ A⁻¹]

Direction:

  • Away from positive charge
  • Toward negative charge

3.2 Relation Between Electric Force and Electric Field

If a charge Q is placed in an electric field E, it experiences a force:

F = Q × E

If Q > 0, force is in the direction of the field.

If Q < 0, force is opposite to the field.

3.3 Superposition of Electric Field

The resultant electric field at any point is the vector sum of the electric fields due to individual charges:

E_net = E₁ + E₂ + E₃ + ...

If E₁ and E₂ are at an angle θ:

Magnitude:

E = √(E₁² + E₂² + 2 × E₁ × E₂ × cosθ)

Direction:

tanα = (E₂ × sinθ) / (E₁ + E₂ × cosθ)

3.4 Electric Field Due to a Point Charge

A point charge Q produces an electric field at a distance r:

E = (1 / (4 × π × ε₀)) × (Q / r²)

For positive Q, field is radially outward.

For negative Q, field is inward.

3.5 Continuous Charge Distributions

Three types of charge densities:

  • Linear charge density: λ = q / L (unit: C/m)
  • Surface charge density: σ = q / A (unit: C/m²)
  • Volume charge density: ρ = q / V (unit: C/m³)

3.6 Properties of Electric Field Lines

  • Field lines start from positive and end on negative charges.
  • Number of lines ∝ magnitude of charge.
  • Number of lines per unit area ∝ field strength.
  • Tangent to a field line gives the direction of E at that point.
  • Field lines never intersect.
  • Uniform fields have straight, parallel, evenly spaced lines.
  • Field lines don’t form closed loops.
  • They emerge and terminate perpendicular to a conductor's surface.
  • Field lines go from higher to lower potential.
  • No field lines in regions with zero electric field.

3.7 Motion of Charged Particle in an Electric Field

(a) Particle Initially at Rest in Uniform Field

Let charge = Q, mass = m, field = E.

Force: F = Q × E

Acceleration: a = F / m = Q × E / m

Velocity after time t: v = a × t = (Q × E × t) / m

Displacement after time t:

Use v² = 2as → v² = (2 × Q × E × s) / m

Momentum: p = m × v = Q × E × t

Kinetic energy:

KE = ½ m v² = (Q² × E² × t²) / (2m)

(b) Particle Enters Field Perpendicularly (Parabolic Path)

Let initial velocity along x-axis = u

x-displacement: x = u × t

y-acceleration: a = Q × E / m

y-displacement:

y = ½ a × t² = (1/2) × (Q × E / m) × t²

So,

y = (Q × E × x²) / (2 × m × u²)

This is the equation of a parabola.

Velocity components:

vₓ = u (constant)

v_y = a × t = (Q × E × t) / m

Resultant velocity:

v = √(vₓ² + v_y²)

Angle with x-axis:

tanβ = v_y / vₓ = (Q × E × t) / (m × u)

4. Electric Dipole

4.1 General Information

A dipole is a system of two equal and opposite charges separated by a small, fixed distance.

  • Dipole axis: Line joining negative to positive charge.
  • Equatorial axis: Perpendicular bisector of the dipole.
  • Dipole length (d): Distance between the two charges.
  • Dipole moment (p): p = q × d (vector from –q to +q)
  • SI unit: coulomb-metre (C·m) or Debye (1 Debye = 3.3 × 10⁻³⁰ C·m)
  • Dimension: [M⁰ L¹ T⁰ A¹]

Note:

  • Region around a stationary dipole has only electric field.
  • In dielectrics, atoms/molecules behave like tiny dipoles.

(a) Electric Potential Due to a Dipole

At point P at distance r, angle θ from axis:

V = (1 / (4 × π × ε₀)) × (p × cosθ) / r²

Where:

  • p = dipole moment
  • θ = angle between dipole axis and position vector

Valid for r >> d

(b) Electric Field Due to Dipole

(i) On the Axial Line:

E = (1 / (4 × π × ε₀)) × (2 × p) / r³

Direction: Along the dipole axis.

(ii) On the Equatorial Line:

E = (1 / (4 × π × ε₀)) × (p) / r³

Direction: Opposite to the dipole moment vector p.

Summary for r >> d:

  • On axial line: E = (1 / (4 × π × ε₀)) × (2p) / r³
  • On equatorial line: E = (1 / (4 × π × ε₀)) × (p) / r³

Both decrease as 1 / r³

4.3 Electric Dipole in Uniform Electric Field

Let dipole p make angle θ with uniform electric field E

(i) Net Force: F_net = 0

(ii) Torque: τ = p × E = p × E × sinθ

This torque tends to align the dipole with the field.

(iii) Work Done: To rotate the dipole from angle θ₁ to θ₂: W = p × E × (cosθ₁ – cosθ₂)

(iv) Potential Energy (U): U = –p × E × cosθ

Minimum potential energy when θ = 0° (aligned with field) ⇒ U = –pE

Maximum when θ = 180° (opposite) ⇒ U = +pE
